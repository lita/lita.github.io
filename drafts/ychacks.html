YC Hacks

Wed 06 August 2014
By Lita Cho

In Blog.

tags: ychackathons

I participated in YC Hacks, the first ever hackathon hosted by Y Combinator. I found out about it through Hacker News and surprised I got in.

I applied without a team, and completely forgot about it till the week of the hackathon. I was about to email the organizers stating I wouldn't go, since I didn't have an idea or a team. However, I am glad I didn't. The night before, I went to a mixer meant for people to find teams. I met Jimmy and Brendan (a fellow hacker schooler), who convince me to go. Jimmy stated that a lot of people applied to get in, and it would be a wasted opportunity if I didn't.

So I decided to go. I found a team. We made a game called Tunes Against Humanity, which is a music spin-off of "Cards Against Humanity". We made it with Firebase and Bop.fm's API. We ended up winning the grand prize for Music Hack from Bop.fm.

I haven't been to many hackathons. But I honestly enjoyed my time. I thought I write up the good parts and the bad parts. Let's start with the bad.

The Bad

Everyone stayed up late

The hackathon took place on Saturday and Sunday. Most people stayed up late and I felt the pressure to stay all night to ship code. I didn't because I didn't want to sacrifice my health for a hackathon, but YC seem to encourage people to stay up.

Crowded

There were some teams who didn't get a table and people were hacking on the floor.

The Good

Office Hours

During the YC Hackathon, we had the opportunity to have office hours with the alumni of YC. Originally, I thought this was get help with our hack. But I found out it we could get general startup advice. I was able to sit in on my teammates office hour and hear some advice from a founder. Everyone there seemed really eager to help each other out, which was surprising. I thought startup incubator would be a lot more cut throat. However, the YC network seems really tight knit, and super willing to help each other.

Now I understand why if you want to start a startup, it helps to be in SF. I don't think you can get that kind of a network anywhere else.

What I learned

It is important to spec your idea before your start coding. I loved that my team talked about functionality and design before coding things out.
